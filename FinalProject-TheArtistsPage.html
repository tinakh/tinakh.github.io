John Gould (1804-1881)

Britain’s leading orinthologist-lithographer since the 1830s. Darwin commissioned him to classify and illustrate the birds during their trip to the Galapagos. Although Darwin relied heavily on Gould’s publications and advice for the bird chapters of the Descent of Man (1871), Gould opposed Darwin’s theories.
Alfred Brehm (1829-1884)

Darwin obtained 11 of the woodblocks from Brehm’s ornithological publication. Reproducing woodblock prints was much cheaper than commissioning someone to copy the wood engravings from Gould’s lithographs. Darwin employed Brehm’s illustrations for the Descent of Man (1871).
T.W. Wood (1839-1910)

Soon after Darwin expressed regret over employing Brehm’s illustrations, Darwin’s commissioned T.W. Wood to re-draw some of Brehm’s birds. T.W. Wood’s illustrations replaced four of Brehm’s. Like Darwin, he was interested in male birds’ display of secondary sexual characteristics for courting a mate.
William De Morgan (1839-1917)

As a potter and designer, De Morgan took a particular interest in designing fantastical animals and birds on his ceramic wares. He worked closely with members of the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood at Morris, Marshall, Faulkner & Co. and was a founding member of the Arts and Crafts Exhibition Society
